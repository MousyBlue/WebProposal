About

ACT Wildlife is a charity organisation aimed at supporting and “[maintaining] the wildlife and biodiversity of the ACT” (ACT Wildlife, 2020). They provide services such as wildlife rescue, community education, and training programs to help people better care for native animals.

Founded in 2013 with little funds or staff, the group slowly gained support in the community and began to grow and expand their range. Currently, ACT wildlife is focused on a project trying to prevent mange in the wombats of Canberra’s Murrumbidgee corridor.

Users

ACT Wildlife and its supporters appear to be most active on their Facebook page, where the post daily animal images and facts, and share important information. Examining the interactions with these post, it appears typical users of this page appear to be females from the 60-75 year age group. These users are companionate and care about the welfare of their local animals, wanting to help those in need. They are also of the age where any children they have had may be grown up, giving them time to pursue other hobbies.

Simply examining the Facebook comments section only gives a small section of the demographics interacting with this organisation, however, as it is limited to those with an active public account on the site. Statistics show that the number of facebook users decreases with each bracket after age 35, with the majority of users being in the 25-34 year old age group. Females are also more likely than males to have an account, with the difference particularly noticeable in older age groups. This means that simply inferring user demographics based of facebook interaction is not possible, as it is limited to those already using the site.

Further information about the users of ACT Wildlife can be found in their newsletters, printed approximately quarterly from 2013-2017. Reading through these, we find most senior members and volunteers to be of a similar older demographic, with a few younger members with specialised wildlife care skills. Junior members (under 18) are also present, however, this particular group may be reliant on their parents for direction and funding.

Aside from the regular subscribed members of ACT Wildlife, a large section off user may simply be casual – those who have happened across an injured animal and are searching for ways to help it, those wanting to donate to a worthy cause, or those wanting to find out a little more about animal care. This covers a very wide range of potential demographics, but their common feature is being new to wildlife care and the charity.

Current Site

The current webpage of ACT Wildlife contains a lot of information about their charity, including backstory, upcoming events, photos, projects, donation links, and more. The home page tries to fit all this information, however, lacks order, hierarchy and clarity. Many links are broken, and images do not display, making it hard to navigate or understand.

Attempt has been made to organise the site into different pages with a navigation bar, but it is unclear which links are clickable and which are simply headings. Many of the pages also contain very little information, so the separation can feel unnecessary.

Priorities for updating the page include:- Restructuring the layout of the homepage to be more cohesive o This includes creating a distinct hierarchy of information, and potentially moving less important info to other pages - Reorganising the navigation bar into more informative headings and subheadings.- Removing or redoing broken or unused links/sections

This moodboard was created using several elements distinct to the organisation
First, is the ACT Wildlife logo. This showcases their motto and mascot, as well as using the same colour scheme that they frequently use in publications.
Next, is the emergency banner from their webpage. I pinned this as its background of pistachio green is one of the "theme" colours of the organisation, and the (rather haphazardly used) text colours are also key site colours. It also neatly expresses their goal: To care for wildlife in the ACT.
As ACT Wildlife focuses on native animals, I gathered a selection of images of animals in their care, including wombats, birds, kangaroos and bats. These highlight both the diversity of wildlife present in the ACT, and the cute faces of those who have been helped by the organisation.
Using the images, logo, and banner, I assembled a colour pallete that would help guide my site design. These colours include green from the original website, greys from the logo, wombats, and kangaroos, yellow for the wattle, and pink for the gallah.
